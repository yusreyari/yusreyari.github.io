  • April 7, 2026

    I started praying 3 days ago. I never actually knew how to pray all this time. I’ve been raised Muslim all my life and this entire time I didn’t know what to say in between reciting the first surah, Al-Fatiha, all the way until the concluding remarks of the prayer, “As-salaamu ‘alaikum wa rohmatul-loh, As-salaamu ‘alaikum wa rohmatul-loh.” All the movements in between: the full 90 degree bow forward, the moment when you’re sitting on your knees, the part when your forehead touches the floor, I would have no idea what to say. I would usually just stand or sit there thinking about literally anything other than praying. No one taught me how to pray and I never went and sought out how. My mother on the other hand loves to pray. Finds comfort in it. She prays all 5 of the daily prayers and even does the extras too. There’s bonus prayers we can do and she does them every night between the hours of midnight and 3AM. She is a faithful and devout woman and has consistently been so, my entire life. She’s the reason I’m “Muslim.” She constantly nagged me to pray and it annoyed the shit out of me. I always found her faithfulness and consistency bewildering. But I realize now, how insensitive I’d been. I never engaged or genuinely questioned why she was on me to pray. I just judged her from afar. I really judged everyone from afar. I’m really quite self-centered and judgmental sometimes. Stuck in my own way. And then a few days ago, I really needed some quiet. I did the thing where you doom scroll for hours while simultaneously thinking in the back of your head all the things you could be doing instead of doom scrolling so you basically just end up with a migraine and an overwhelming sense of how much of a chud you are. I googled, “how to pray: muslim.” I found a little cheat sheet. I walked to the bathroom and made wudhu (ritualistic cleansing of head, arms, feet before prayer). I unfurled the sajada (prayer rug). I put on a white peci (male Muslim prayer cap) and wrapped a sarong (robe to cover ankles when you’re wearing shorts) around my waist. And I did it. I did it thoroughly. I looked at my phone at each movement to make sure I was reciting the correct lines. And at the end of the prayer, I just sat there. In silence. Like a wave of calmness washed over me. The haziness and self-loathing vanished. I felt refreshed. It was really jarring. All these feelings of annoyance and judgment about prayer, about my Mom, about everyone at the Mosque had vanished. It was like everything that happened before I prayed didn’t matter anymore. That I could just restart. And move forward and try again. The cleansing of the body. Wearing the peci and wrapping a sarong. Reciting the Arabic. The simple movements with a predictable beginning, middle, and end. The ritual of it all. Why does it work? Because it doesn’t come from my belief or faith in Allah (God). Sorry Allah (swt). Is it something physical? Something with the body? Is it my mother’s love? Does it even matter why it works? Do I have to know why? Do I always have to come up with a logical explanation for everything? Maybe another time. But for now, this works. I feel peace.

    I’m not sure why, but I’ve been feeling so miserable these past few days. I think it has to do with something about the family. Today I saw them outside, hanging by the pool. Instead of going outside and joining them or waving at them from my window, I went and sat in the chair where I knew they wouldn’t see me. I didn’t want to interact with them. Why? Why can’t I speak or why do I choose not to interact with them? Why do I feel so uncomfortable around them? Is it their wealth? Is it all of the judgment I have for them? Have I typecast them? I hate being so ungrateful. But when I’m at their house, I don’t want to really see them. Why? I feel like I’m not meeting their expectations. But what are their expectations of me? Or what do I think their expectations of me are? That I find the value in their creations. That I provide added value to their institutions. That I report back that what they’ve worked on/are working on are indeed incredible and amazing and good for the world. But I don’t necessarily find value in their work. Why? Well I do think YCAB’s signature program, the microloans to the mothers of the urban poor, to be the most substantial work that they produce. But what about the rest? Why can’t I accept them for who they are? How do I become more accepting of this elite family? But I cannot find myself praising them, like everyone else. It is easier for me to praise Mr. Beeken and Pak A than to praise the work of this family. I find the value they bring to the world. But why do I feel like this? Why? Returning to the house feels like returning to judgment. Or to emptiness. Returning to nothing at all. There isn’t anyone waiting for me. Or anyone to speak to me. And even if there was, what would we even speak about? There are no shared interests. We don’t share anything. I am but a leach to this family. Some non-human entity. That brings nothing. They can feel it from me too. My lack. Maybe even my ungratefulness. They don’t know what I’m up to, which makes things worse. But I feel like even if they did know what I was up to, it wouldn’t be of interest to them. In my head, they would respect my time spent if it was spent reading the bible, learning how to use AI, starting my own business, or taking Mandarin lessons. I’m starting to feel like I can’t endure this living situation. That I feel peace at the house, when it’s empty. When they are away. When they are travelling. I don’t know how to play poker. Or even care about padel. Or pokemon cards. But my joy and my adventures are subsidized by them. They’ve probably spent close to $5,000 USD on me. That’s another thing that weighs on me. My debt. My debt to them grows by the day. I will heed their call, for the rest of my life. I will jump for them, every time they ask. What do I do? Where should I live? That’s why I’ve got to move back. Or how do I find a way to want to live there, here again. How do I find it?
